# Brian Tracy Copy Rules — "Sell the Destination, Not the Plane"

> Use this file as a reference when rewriting website copy, proposals, emails, or any client-facing text.
> Every line should answer: **"What's in it for ME (the buyer)?"**

---

## The Core Rule

The buyer doesn't care about your plane (features, team, process).
They care about the destination (their transformed future state).

| ❌ Plane (about you) | ✅ Destination (about them) |
|---|---|
| "We do X" | "You get Y" |
| "Our team has 20 years of experience" | "You get results in weeks, not quarters" |
| "We build AI models that..." | "Imagine [specific better future]..." |
| "50+ projects delivered" | "€200M+ revenue impact created" |

---

## 7 Rewrite Rules

### 1. Flip the subject from "We" to "You"

Every sentence should start with or center on the buyer. If a sentence starts with "We", "Our", or your company name — rewrite it.

- ❌ "We help organizations turn data into results"
- ✅ "Get a clear AI roadmap, working solutions, and real ROI"

### 2. Sell the outcome, not the capability

Don't describe what you can do. Describe what the buyer's life looks like after.

- ❌ "Dynamic pricing that adapts to real behavior"
- ✅ "Higher margins without losing competitiveness"
- ❌ "We build practical governance models"
- ✅ "Everyone knows who owns what — no more finger-pointing"

### 3. Use "Imagine" and "What if" to paint the future

When describing solutions, put the buyer in a vivid future state. Make it specific and concrete.

- ❌ "We turn fragmented data into real insight"
- ✅ "Imagine your sales team walking into every meeting fully briefed — knowing the customer's history, open issues, and next likely need"

### 4. Move credibility signals to proof, not headlines

Your experience, team size, and years in business are proof points — not headlines. They belong in stats strips, footers, or case studies. The hero and section headers belong to the buyer's transformation.

- **Hero headline:** The buyer's desired outcome
- **Stats strip:** Your track record framed as their peers' results
- **Case studies:** Proof that the destination is real

### 5. Name the pain they're escaping

The most powerful copy pairs the destination with the pain they're leaving behind. Use contrast.

- ✅ "...without the months of discovery, junior consultants, or shelf documents you've experienced before"
- ✅ "Results in weeks, not quarters"
- ✅ "Your team starts executing — not waiting for permission"

Pattern: **[Positive outcome] — not/without/instead of [pain they know]**

### 6. Make outcomes concrete and board-ready

Vague outcomes don't sell. Specific, measurable, presentable outcomes do. The buyer needs to visualize showing this to their boss or board.

- ❌ "Measurable business results"
- ✅ "Your AI investments show up in your P&L, not just your slide deck"
- ❌ "Create compelling business cases"
- ✅ "Walk into the board meeting with an answer, not a question"

### 7. Make every process step about what THEY experience

When describing how you work, each step should describe the buyer's experience and progress — not your internal process.

- ❌ "A focused session to define the problem, map the data, and outline the approach"
- ✅ "You leave with a defined problem, a clear data picture, and an approach you believe in"
- ❌ "We deliver working solutions, not slide decks"
- ✅ "You have something working, something measurable, and something you can show your board"

---

## Quick Self-Check

Before publishing any client-facing copy, run through this checklist:

- [ ] Does the headline describe the buyer's transformation, not your service category?
- [ ] Do more sentences start with "You/Your" than "We/Our"?
- [ ] Are outcomes concrete enough to present to a board?
- [ ] Is there a pain-to-gain contrast in key sections?
- [ ] Are credibility signals (years, team, projects) in proof sections, not headlines?
- [ ] Could a buyer read this and see themselves in a better future?
- [ ] Does the CTA describe what the buyer will know/have after taking action?

## Section-by-Section Patterns

### Hero
- **Headline:** Buyer's transformation in ≤10 words
- **Sub-headline:** What they get + what they escape (1–2 sentences max)

### Stats Strip
- Frame numbers as peer outcomes, not your resume
- "€200M+ revenue impact created" > "50+ projects delivered"
- Speed > experience ("Results in weeks" > "20+ years experience")

### Solution/Service Cards
- **Hook:** Start with the pain they already feel (keep existing pain hooks if good)
- **Body:** "Imagine..." or "What if..." followed by specific future state
- **Outcomes:** 3 bullets, each starting with what the buyer experiences

### How It Works / Process
- Every step starts with "You" — the buyer progresses through the journey
- "You know... You decide... You have..."

### Why Us / Differentiators
- Reframe as "What working with us feels like"
- Each point = their experience, not your feature
- ❌ "Senior people, not junior pyramids"
- ✅ "You work with the experts who do the work — no hand-off to juniors"

### CTA
- Describe what the buyer will know or have after taking action
- ❌ "Book a free consultation"
- ✅ "In 30 minutes, you'll know your single highest-impact AI opportunity"
- Always end with a risk-reducer: "No pitch, no pressure"

---

## Tone Notes

- Confident but not arrogant — the buyer is the hero, you're the guide
- Short sentences hit harder than long ones
- Contrasts create energy: "not X, but Y" / "instead of X, you get Y"
- Avoid jargon unless the buyer uses it themselves
- If you can't explain the outcome in one sentence, you don't understand it yet
